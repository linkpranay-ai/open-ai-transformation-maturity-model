## Purpose

This guide exists so that people using the assessment framework don’t invent their own scoring logic. Without it, results from different teams are basically impossible to compare, because everyone has a slightly different idea of what a “high” or “low” score means.

The goal isn’t to be ultra-precise — just consistent enough that the numbers actually tell you something useful.

## Scoring Scale

Each item gets a score between 0 and 4:

- **0** — not present at all  
- **1** — shows up here and there, mostly experimental  
- **2** — repeatable in some groups, but not widespread  
- **3** — common practice across a large portion of the org  
- **4** — the normal way things run, backed by oversight  

If you’re unsure whether something is a 2 or a 3, it’s usually safer to choose the lower one. Scores tend to drift upward otherwise.

## Scoring Layers

Don’t compress everything into a single number — that usually hides more than it reveals.

### 1. Level-Fit Score
How closely everyday behavior matches a maturity level. This is about reality on the ground, not formal documentation.

### 2. Pillar Score
Strength in each capability area. Big differences between pillars are normal and often highlight where effort should go next.

### 3. Gate Score
Whether the minimum conditions are in place to move forward safely. Skipping this check is how organizations end up with sophisticated tools and brittle systems.

## Interpretation Guidance

Averages alone can be misleading.

For instance, strong AI adoption combined with weak testing or governance might still produce a respectable overall score. In practice, that situation is fragile — productivity gains can disappear quickly when something goes wrong.

Look for weak spots and unevenness; those usually matter more than the headline number.

## Recommended Use

This framework works best when repeated periodically rather than used once and forgotten.

Common uses include:

- regular maturity reviews (quarterly works for many orgs)  
- planning sessions to prioritize improvements  
- comparing different teams internally  
- benchmarking against peers, when data is available  
- tracking progress over time  

Don’t expect perfectly smooth improvement. Real change tends to come in bursts.
